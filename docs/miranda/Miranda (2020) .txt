Abstract
Comparative genomics is a field of biology that aims at comparing genomes of different species. One major question of this field is to find the evolutionary distance between
two given genomes. One way to estimate such distance is to use the rearrangement
distance, which consists in finding a minimum cost sequence of rearrangements that
transforms one genome into another. We use permutations to model the genomes being
compared and, in this way, we can treat this problem as the problem of sorting a permutation with a minimum cost sequence of rearrangements. In the early works with
rearrangement distance, it was considered that all rearrangements are equally likely to
occur and, consequently, they use a unitary cost for all rearrangements. Some variations of the problem were motivated by the observation that rearrangements involving
large segments of a genome rarely occur. One of these variants also uses a unitary
cost, however it adds a constraint in the length of the operations allowed to estimate the distance. Another variant uses a cost function based on the rearrangement’s
length. In this work, we study problems that combine both variants, that is, problems
with a constraint in the rearrangement’s length and with a cost function based on the
rearrangement’s length. We present approximation algorithms for five such problems
involving reversals and/or transpositions for sorting signed and unsigned permutations. We also analyze the problems for specific parameters of the length restriction
and for when the cost function is equal to α , where  is the rearrangement’s length
and α ≥ 1 is a real value parameter.
Keywords Genome rearrangements · Approximation algorithms · Sorting
permutations

1 Introduction
One of the main challenges in Computational Biology is to estimate the evolutionary
distance among species, considering that new organisms arise from mutations that
occurred on the genetic material of other organisms. One way to estimate such distance
is to consider the rearrangement distance between the organisms’ genomes we are
comparing. Such distance consists in the cost of the sequence of rearrangements that
occurred in the transformation of one genome into the other.
A genome rearrangement is a large scale mutation which alters stretches of a
genome. Two of the most studied genome rearrangements are reversals and transpositions. A reversal reverses the order of the genes in a segment of the genome,
while a transposition exchanges two adjacent segments. We define the length of a
rearrangement as the number of elements affected by it.
A genome can be represented as an ordered set of genes. A synteny block is a region
with high similarity between two distinct genomes, and it can be a single gene or a
sequence of genes. When we want to compare two genomes, we represent a genome as
a sequence of synteny blocks (Dias 2012). Assuming that there are no repeated genes,
this representation is modeled as a permutation of integers. Furthermore, genes and
blocks have orientations and, when this orientation is known, the genome is modeled as
a signed permutation, where the sign of an element indicates the orientation of the gene
or block. When the orientation is unknown, we simply use an unsigned permutation
to represent a genome.
When using permutations, the genome rearrangement problems consist in finding a minimum cost sequence of rearrangements which transforms one permutation
into another. We can always represent one of the genomes as the identity permutation (sorted permutation) and, in this way, these problems are equivalent to sorting a
permutation with a minimum cost sequence of rearrangements.
The problems of Sorting Permutations by Reversals and Sorting Permutations by
Transpositions are NP-hard (Caprara 1999; Bulteau et al. 2012). The best result for
both problems is a 1.375-approximation algorithm (Berman et al. 2002; Elias and
Hartman 2006). When considering only signed permutations, the problem of Sorting
Permutations by Reversals becomes solvable in polynomial time (Hannenhalli and
Pevzner 1999). The problem of Sorting Permutations by (Signed or Unsigned) Reversals and Transpositions is also NP-hard (Oliveira et al. 2019). For this problem, the best
results are a 2-approximation algorithm (Walter et al. 1998) for signed permutations
and a 2k-approximation algorithm (Rahman et al. 2008) for unsigned permutations,
where k is the approximation factor for cycle decomposition of breakpoints graphs.
Using the best-known value of k (Chen 2013), this approximation factor becomes
equal to 2.8334 + , for  > 0.
All the problems mentioned above consider the traditional approach of sorting
permutations by rearrangements problems. In this approach, all rearrangements are
considered to have the same probability to happen and, consequently, the rearrangement distance is equal to the minimum number of rearrangements that sort a
permutation. However, studies show that some rearrangements are more likely to
occur than others (Bader and Ohlebusch 2007; Blanchette et al. 1996) and, therefore,
a weighted approach is more appropriate to estimate the evolutionary distance. In the

literature, there are cost functions based on the rearrangement’s length (Pinter and

The problem of Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted Rearrangements was
motivated by the observation that, in some species, the rearrangements that occurred
in the evolutionary process do not act on very large regions (Blanchette et al. 1996;
Lefebvre et al. 2003). In this variation, the cost of a rearrangement β is defined by the
function f (β) = |β|α , where |β| is the length of β and α > 0 is a real value parameter.
For α = 1, the Sorting by Length-Weighted (Signed or Unsigned) Reversals has a
O(lg n)-approximation algorithm (Bender et al. 2008). Considering transpositions and
the combination of (signed or unsigned) reversals and transpositions, the best known
results are O(lg2 n)-approximation algorithms (Lintzmayer et al. 2015). For α ≥ 2,
there are algorithms with constant approximation factor for all variations (Bender
et al. 2008; Lintzmayer et al. 2015). Furthermore, for α ≥ 3, the problems involving
unsigned permutations become solvable in polynomial time (Bender et al. 2008).
Another variant of the genome rearrangement problems, where there exists a constraint in the length of the allowed rearrangements, arose from the same motivation
of the length-weighted variation. Given an integer λ > 1, only rearrangements of
length less than or equal to λ are allowed to modify a permutation in the problems of
Sorting Permutations by λ-Rearrangements. For any value of λ and any combination
of rearrangements (reversals and/or transpositions) for signed and unsigned permutations, there are O(λ2 )-approximation algorithms (Miranda et al. 2019). For λ = 3,
there exist algorithms with constant approximation factor (Galvão et al. 2015; Vergara
1998). For λ = 2, these problems are solvable in polynomial time (Jerrum 1985;
Galvão et al. 2015).
Nguyen et al. (2005) considered the problem of Sorting Length-Weighted Signed
λ-Reversals, which combines the length constraint with the cost function based on the
length of the rearrangements. They presented a O(lg n)-approximation, for λ = Ω(n)
and α = 1, and a (2 lg2 n + lg n)-approximation, for λ = o(n) and α = 1.
In this work, we study the problems of Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted
λ-Rearrangements, which are named as: Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted λReversals (SbR); Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted λ-Transpositions (SbT);
Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted λ-Reversals and λ-Transpositions (SbRT);
Sorting Permutations by Length-Weighted Signed λ-Reversals (SbR̄); and Sorting
Permutations by Length-Weighted Signed λ-Reversals and λ-Transpositions (SbR̄T).
We start by considering α = 1 and any value of λ. After this, yet considering
α = 1, we present results (i) for large values of λ and (ii) for λ = 3. A rearrangement
of length at most 3 is also known as a short rearrangement (Heath and Vergara 2003;
Galvão et al. 2015). At last, we consider that α > 1 and analyze how this affects the
rearrangement distance.
The rest of this work is organized as follows. Section 2 presents definitions related
to the problems. Sections 3 and 4 describe algorithms for unsigned permutations
and Sect. 5 describes algorithms for signed permutations. Section 6 presents new

2 Deﬁnitions
A signed permutation of length n is represented as π = (π1 π2 . . . πn ), such that
πi ∈ {−n, . . . , −2, −1, +1, +2, . . . , +n} and |πi | = |π j | if and only if i = j,
for all i and j. An unsigned permutation of length n is represented similarly, but
πi ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n} and πi = π j if and only if i = j, for all i and j. The identity
permutation, ι = (1 2 . . . n), is the goal of the sorting problems we will study. For
signed permutations, all elements of ι have positive sign.
A signed reversal ρ̄(i, j), with 1 ≤ i ≤ j ≤ n, reverts the segment πi , πi+1 , . . . ,
π j and flips the sign of each affected element. An unsigned reversal ρ(i, j),
with 1 ≤ i < j ≤ n, only reverts the segment πi , πi+1 , . . . , π j . So, when π
is a signed permutation, we have that (π1 . . . πi−1 πi . . . π j π j+1 . . . πn ) ·
ρ̄(i, j) = (π1 . . . πi−1 −π j . . . −πi π j+1 . . . πn ) and, when π is an unsigned
permutation, we have that (π1 . . . πi−1 πi . . . π j π j+1 . . . πn ) · ρ(i, j) =
(π1 . . . πi−1 π j . . . πi π j+1 . . . πn ). The length of a signed reversal ρ̄(i, j) or
an unsigned reversal ρ(i, j) is equal to |ρ̄(i, j)| = |ρ(i, j)| = j − i + 1.
A transposition τ (i, j, k), with 1 ≤ i < j < k ≤ n + 1, exchanges the
two adjacent segments πi , πi+1 , . . . , π j−1 and π j , π j+1 , . . . , πk−1 without changing signs of their elements. Therefore, we have that (π1 . . . πi−1 πi . . . π j−1
π j . . . πk−1 πk . . . πn ) · τ (i, j, k) = (π1 . . . πi−1 π j . . . πk−1 πi . . . π j−1
πk . . . πn ). A transposition τ (i, j, k) has length |τ (i, j, k)| = k − i.
Given an integer λ > 1, we say that a rearrangement is a λ-rearrangement if its
length is less than or equal to λ. Furthermore, we say that a rearrangement β is super
short if |β| ≤ 2 and short if |β| ≤ 3.

2.1 Inversions
A pair of elements (πi , π j ) of π is called an inversion if i < j and |πi | > |π j |. We use
Inv(π ) to denote the number of inversions in a permutation π . For any permutation π ,
Inv(π ) = 0 if and only if |π1 | < |π2 | < · · · < |πn |. Note that considering unsigned
permutations, only the identity permutation has Inv(π ) = 0.
Example 1 The permutation π = (3 4 1 2) has the inversions (π1 = 3, π3 = 1),
(π1 = 3, π4 = 2), (π2 = 4, π3 = 1), and (π2 = 4, π4 = 2). In this example,
Inv(π ) = 4.
Lemma 1 For any permutation π , if Inv(π ) > 0, then there exists an inversion
(πi , πi+1 ).
Proof Let π1 , . . . , πi be a maximal subsequence such that |π j | < |π j+1 | for all
1 ≤ j < i. Since Inv(π ) > 0, we have that i < n. So, |πi | > |πi+1 | and (πi , πi+1 ) is
an inversion.

Given a permutation π and a rearrangement β, let ΔInv(π, β) = Inv(π )−Inv(π ·β),
that is, ΔInv(π, β) denotes the change caused by β in the number of inversions in π .
From Lemma 1, we can conclude that for any permutation π such that Inv(π ) > 0,
there exists a super short rearrangement β with ΔInv(π, β) = 1.
The permutation graph or inversion graph of a permutation π is the undirected
graph G(π ) = (V , E), where V = {π1 , π2 , . . . , πn } and E = {(πi , π j ) : the pair
(πi , π j ) is an inversion in π }. Figure 1 shows an example of a permutation graph.
Given a signed permutation π , a connected component C of G(π ) is odd if it
contains an odd number of negative elements (vertices) and it is even otherwise. We
use c(π ) and codd (π ) to denote the number of connected components and the number of
odd connected components of G(π ), respectively. An edge e of G(π ) whose deletion
increases the number of connected components of G(π ) is called a cut-edge. We use
γ (π ) to denote the number of non-isolated vertices in G(π ), that is, the number of
elements in π belonging to at least one inversion.

rearrangements that most reduce the number of inversions with the lowest cost.

7 Length-weighted short rearrangement distance
One well studied variant of the rearrangement problems is the one considering
only short rearrangements. The problems of Sorting by Super Short Rearrangements
(Reversals or Transpositions) are polynomial (Jerrum 1985; Galvão et al. 2015). The
problems of Sorting Signed and Unsigned Permutations by Short Reversals have
approximation algorithms with factors 5 (Galvão et al. 2015) and 2 (Heath and Vergara
2003), respectively. The best-known algorithm for the problem of Sorting (Unsigned)
Permutations by Short Transpositions has approximation factor of 5/4 (Jiang et al.
2014). For this problem, there also exists a (1 + n/Inv(π ))-approximation (Jiang et al.
2012), where n is the number of elements in the permutation. For the problems of
Sorting Signed and Unsigned Permutations by Short Reversals and Short Transpositions, there exist algorithms with approximation factors of 3 (Galvão et al. 2015) and
2 (Vergara 1998), respectively.

Lemma 25 For any signed permutation π , if a signed reversal or transposition β with
length 2 removes an inversion of π , then codd (π ) ≤ codd (π · β), that is, the number of
odd components of the inversion graph does not decrease.
Proof Consider that β is a signed reversal. Let πi and πi+1 be the elements affected
by this reversal. Since only these two elements are affected, the inversion removed
by β is (πi , πi+1 ). Let e = (πi , πi+1 ) be the removed edge of G(π ) and C be the
component containing e. We divide our analysis into two cases:
1. e is a cut-edge: let C1 and C2 be the components of C − e in G(π ). Since e is a
cut-edge, the elements πi and πi+1 are in distinct components of C − e. If C is odd,
then either C1 or C2 is odd. Suppose, without loss of generality, that C1 is odd and
C2 is even. After applying the signed reversal, the component C1 becomes even
and the component C2 becomes odd. In this way, the number of odd components
remains the same. If C is even, then both C1 and C2 have the same parity. After
applying the signed reversal, the number of odd components can only increase. In
both cases, we have codd (π ) ≤ codd (π · β).
2. e is not a cut-edge: in this case, the component C has the same set of vertices and
its parity remains the same. Therefore, codd (π ) = codd (π · β).
The proof is analogous for a transposition of length 2.

Lemma 26 For any signed permutation π , if Inv(π ) > 0, then there exists a rearrangement β of length 2 such that Inv(π · β) = Inv(π ) − 1 and codd (π ) = codd (π · β).
Proof Since Inv(π ) > 0, there exists an edge e = (πi , πi+1 ) in G(π ). Let C be
the component that contains e. Suppose that e is not a cut-edge. A transposition
β = τ (i, i + 1, i + 2) removes the inversion (πi , πi+1 ) and, since e is not a cut-edge,
the component C has the same set of vertices and its parity remains the same, as a
transposition does not change signs of elements. So, we have Inv(π · β) = Inv(π ) − 1
and codd (π ) = codd (π · β).
Now, suppose that e is a cut-edge. Let C1 and C2 be the components of C − e. We
further divide this analysis into the following cases:
1. C1 and C2 are both even. After applying β = τ (i, i + 1, i + 2) on π , we have that
Inv(π · β) = Inv(π ) − 1 and codd (π ) = codd (π · β). Note that C is also even and
the parity of the components C1 and C2 remains the same.

the cost of S is greater than or equal to codd (π ) and f (S) ≥ 2α Inv(π ) + codd (π ).

9 Conclusion
In this work, we presented O(λ)-approximation algorithms for the problems of Sorting
by Length-Weighted λ-Rearrangements with α = 1. These algorithms were based on
the concepts of inversions and/or entropy. For large values of λ (i.e. λ = Θ(n)),
we were able to improve the approximation factor to O(lg n), for reversals, and
O(lg2 n), for transpositions and the combination of reversals and transpositions. Also,
we presented algorithms with constant approximation factor for length-weighted short
rearrangements, still considering α = 1.
For α ≥ 2, we gave a 2-approximation algorithm for sorting unsigned permutations
and a 3-approximation algorithm for sorting signed permutations. When α ≥ 3, we
proved that, for each of the five rearrangements models studied in this work, the problem becomes solvable in polynomial time. The best approximation factors obtained
for each problem are shown in Table 1.
For future work, we aim at improving the approximation factor when considering
α = 1 and any value of λ. Besides that, there are other variations of these problems to
be considered which allow rearrangements such as insertions and deletions of genes.
In this case, the genomes may not have the same set of genes.
